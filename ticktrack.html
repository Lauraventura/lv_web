Tick-track allows you to stablish GPS located alarms so that whenever you run by a place where you have some unfinished business you can always recall it.

Research

This project began with a research about people's painpoints surrounding memory issues. The main problem is not about anything you would note in your agenda, it's about all those things that can't be adjusted to timetables, just because you don't know the exact time you'll be able to do them, or because you need to be in a specific place to fulfill a task.

At this point, I began to look into all the resources people used to remind themselves about things. They went from notes in the palm of their hand, to combining 10 apps hoping to look at one of them at the correct time.

Seeing the results of my research, I was able to define more precisely who my person was. She is a very active person, who needs to optimize her time but also have a peace of mind knowing that everything she has to do, will ultimately be done and will not end at the bottom of an endless to-do-list. Being such a busy person makes her a bit forgetful, and she gets stressed by its consequences.

Defining the MVP

Taking into consideration all previous research and focusing on my persona, I could define the features of my MVP: setting an alarm linked to a place, and defining favourite places to favour a more agile workflow. Those were the features I thought would make an interesting MVP, showing the main value this app can offer.

But of course, I can expect more growth from this product. I also defined the next steps to make this app a perfect product for my persona: Sharing alarms, creating alarms that sound when you approach a specific person… But those features require specific testing and technical investigation

Work flows

Flows were really important in this project, as they directly affected the structure of the app. Users would mainly open the app in two situations: to set a new alarm, or to to see the exact position of an alarm once they have received a notification (and then check it). Those two options had to be there at first sight, and I built all the structure having that priority in mind.

Wireframes

Once I did the first user tests with the low fidelity prototypes, I understood very quickly that, as a productivity and navigation product, it needed to stay very structured. That’s why I considered the MVP had to stick to formulas we know for a fact are recognizable to everyone: I used a fixed navigation bar at the bottom of the screen to access all sections.

UI

So, I had built a super structured product, I didn’t need to do a super flashy UI, but one that allowed me to emphasise the hierarchy of contents I had built. I made a moodboard to set a visual tone, but also to explore and experiment with gradients, brightness and color palettes to make some elements pop without making a crazy UI. You can appreciate the process and all the experimentation in the images.

Prototype

You can finally see the prototype of the app. I have integrated the Google Maps API because users are very used to its navigation, but also to have information about the places you search, such as business hours, so that you have the possibility to set an alarm that is only active when that business is open.
